Opening Words

I have written many essays for marks and many because the syllabus demanded it. This one is different. This essay is a letter of gratitude, a record of a shift in thinking, and a quiet celebration of a course that did more than transfer information. It rearranged the furniture of my mind.

I am a student of Neural and Cognitive Sciences, immersed daily in neurons, synapses, and research papers that demand evidence, p-values, and methodological rigor. I chose the Anthrogogy elective expecting a break from hard science, a gentle philosophical interlude before the intensity of my thesis. I did not expect a transformation. But that is precisely what happened, and I need to write it down before the semester fades and the feeling dims.

This is not a typical academic paper. It is an attempt to trace the invisible lines of my own learning, to map a territory I didn’t know existed until someone handed me a blank page and said, “Draw.”

A Word of Gratitude

Gratitude is a word we use lightly, often as a social reflex. But what I feel after this semester goes beyond politeness. It is a deep, quiet recognition that something rare has happened, and that it happened because of specific people and specific choices.

I want to say something at the start that I will repeat at the end: I am grateful.

I am grateful to the University of Hyderabad for offering an elective that prioritizes depth over coverage and meaning over marks. In an academic system that often rewards memorization and punishes deviation, this course felt like an anomaly, a gift. It asked not “What do you remember?” but “What do you notice about your own thinking?” That question is radical. It is almost subversive. And yet, here it was, tucked inside a regular semester, treated as normal.

And I am grateful to Prof. Shree Deepa. I first encountered her last semester in a course on Indian Knowledge Systems. I remember walking into that classroom expecting another lecture series. Instead, I found a teacher who paused after asking a question and genuinely waited. Not the performative pause that teachers do before answering themselves, but a real, open silence that communicated: Your thoughts matter here. She taught with a rare openness, curious about what we thought, not just whether we had understood. When I learned she was offering Anthrogogy, I did not hesitate even though my thesis carried 20 credits and every rational calculation said I should take something lighter. I chose her class because of how she made me think. That decision, small but deliberate, was my first act of adult learning: choosing based on what mattered, not what was easiest.

Before Anthrogogy: The Invisible Cage

Before this course, I believed learning was a straightforward transaction. There was a teacher at the front of the room, a pre-written syllabus, and students who received, stored, and reproduced information. This model had surrounded me for sixteen years, from my first day of school to my postgraduate seminars. It was as invisible to me as water to a fish. I didn’t question it because I didn’t know I could.

I remember a moment from Class 12 physics. We were studying optics, and the teacher wrote a complex lens formula on the board. I raised my hand and asked, “Why does this formula work? Where does it come from?” He looked at me, not unkindly, and said, “That’s not in the syllabus. We need to move on. Just memorize it for the exam.” I wasn’t angry then. I simply learned that some questions were not welcome. The boundaries of acceptable curiosity were drawn by someone else, somewhere else, for reasons I didn’t understand.

That was pedagogy in its purest form: someone else decides what counts as knowledge, what sequence it should be delivered in, and what questions are worth asking. The learner’s job is to receive without resistance, to internalize without interrogation. I became very good at this. I earned marks, certificates, degrees. But I had never been asked to examine the container I was swimming in. I didn’t know it was a container at all. This course named that container. And by naming it, it made it visible. Once something is visible, you can choose whether to stay inside it or step out.

What Anthrogogy Taught Me Instead

The word anthrogogy comes from the Greek anthropos (adult human) and agogos (guide). It is, at its simplest, the study of how adults actually learn, as distinct from children. But the distinction runs far deeper than age. It touches the very architecture of how we understand knowledge, authority, and ourselves.

The core shift was this: adults are not empty vessels waiting to be filled. We arrive with years of lived experience, deeply held assumptions, invisible thinking habits, and identities shaped by culture, family, and previous education. Anthrogogy says: do not bypass that, use it. The richest learning does not come from receiving new information passively. It comes from bringing your entire self, your successes, your failures, your contradictions, into dialogue with new ideas and noticing what shifts.

In practice, every class session felt less like a lecture and more like a guided excavation. Prof. Deepa would introduce a concept, say, the difference between reacting and responding, and then ask us to find it in our own lives. Not hypothetically. Not in an abstract case study. In the texture of our actual days. What happened yesterday when someone criticized you? What did you feel in your body? What words did you reach for? Could you have chosen differently? These questions turned the classroom into a laboratory where the subject under investigation was myself.

The second major shift was my understanding of autonomy. Before this course, I thought autonomy meant freedom from external control: no deadlines, no grades, no one telling me what to do. It was a negative definition, autonomy as absence of constraint. But Prof. Deepa posed a question that stopped me cold:

“If you are autonomous, why do you still react instead of explore?”

I sat with that question for days. I realized that having freedom does not automatically mean I make reflective, deliberate decisions. Often, I react out of habit, fear, or the invisible scripts I internalized years ago. True autonomy, I began to understand, is not the absence of external rules. It is the presence of internal discernment. It is the responsibility to pause between stimulus and response and choose thoughtfully, not automatically.

A third insight emerged more slowly, through weeks of reflection and conversation. I began to see that much of my “learning” had been performance. I knew how to produce the right answer in the right format at the right time. But that performance and genuine understanding are not the same thing. I could score highly on an exam about ethics while treating a classmate unkindly. I could write a paper on cognitive biases while remaining completely blind to my own. The gap between knowing and being, between intellectual comprehension and lived transformation, became painfully visible. Anthrogogy doesn’t allow you to stay in that gap comfortably. It keeps pointing at it until you decide to do something about it.

The Five Principles of Speech: A Mirror for the Mind

One of the most personally transformative elements of the course was our engagement with the five principles of speech drawn from Indian traditions: Satyam (truth), Hitam (benefit), Priyam (pleasantness), Madhuram (sweetness), and Mitham (moderation). Initially, I approached these as ethical guidelines for communication with others: be truthful, be helpful, be kind, don’t ramble. That seemed straightforward enough.

But Prof. Deepa extended the frame inward. She asked:

“Do you apply these principles to how you speak to yourself?”

The question was jarring. I had never considered my internal monologue as a form of speech that could be truthful or untruthful, beneficial or harmful, gentle or harsh. I started paying attention. What I found was uncomfortable. My inner voice was often critical, exaggerating failures and minimizing successes. It used absolute language: “You always mess this up,” “You never learn.” It was neither truthful, nor beneficial, nor pleasant, nor measured.

Learning to speak to myself differently became a daily practice. When I noticed harsh self-talk, I would pause and ask: Is this true? Is it beneficial? Is there a kinder way to say it that doesn’t sacrifice honesty? The shift was subtle at first, but its effects accumulated. I became less reactive, less defensive, more willing to acknowledge mistakes without collapsing into self-criticism. This was not just a communication skill. It was a reorientation of my relationship with myself.

Real-Life Example One: The Bike Slip

One afternoon, I was riding my bike near Lake View Guest House on campus. The road was familiar; I had taken it a hundred times. But that day, there was loose gravel on a turn I didn’t see. The bike slipped. The fall was minor: a small cut on my palm, a few scratches on my knee, nothing serious. I got up, dusted myself off, and continued to my destination.

But for the next hour, something else was happening inside my head. A voice was replaying the incident on a loop, and every replay added a layer of judgment: You were careless. You should have seen it. You’re always in a hurry. You’re irresponsible. By the time I reached my room, I had turned a minor accident into a full-blown character indictment. I felt heavy, incompetent, ashamed.

Then something unusual happened. I caught myself. I remembered the assignment I had just completed on language potentiality, the idea that words don’t simply describe reality; they actively construct it. The language I choose shapes the experience I have. And here I was, using harsh, absolutist language to narrate a simple event, and the narrative was producing suffering entirely out of proportion to the event itself.

I decided to try an experiment. I sat down and wrote the same incident again, this time with deliberate care: You were driving at a reasonable speed. The gravel was hidden by shadows. You lost traction, which happens to experienced riders too. You are safe. The bike is fine. You learned to check for gravel on turns. This was not a failure; it was a data point.

The difference was immediate and physical. My shoulders relaxed. The tightness in my chest dissolved. The facts had not changed, but the framing had transformed the emotional reality completely. That was the moment I understood, not as theory but as lived experience, that the principles we were studying applied first and most urgently to the voice inside my own head.

A reminder that even minor slips can teach profound lessons.

Real-Life Example Two: The Assignment That Failed (and Taught Me Everything)

The Internal-1 assignment for the course was deceptively open-ended: identify three incidents from your life where pedagogy was at work, and produce a creative output that captures the shift from pedagogy to anthrogogy. No further instructions. No rubric. No template. Just that invitation and the trust that we would find our way.

I decided to make a short film. I titled it in my mind: The Map That Was Never Given. The concept was clear. A printed, official-looking map would represent pedagogy: someone else’s route, pre-drawn, non-negotiable. A hand-drawn map, imperfect and evolving, would represent anthrogogy. The film would follow a student (me) receiving the printed map, struggling with it, discarding it, and slowly, tentatively, drawing his own.

I wrote the script over two days. It was good. It had scenes I could visualize clearly: the classroom, the frustration, the moment of clarity under a tree on campus. I was excited. This was going to be something I could be proud of.

Then reality arrived. I had no fellowship, no budget. I downloaded three different AI video generation tools. Every single one required a paid subscription after a brief trial. I tried free alternatives. They produced unusable, glitchy output. I spent three weeks in this cycle: hope, download, disappointment, uninstall. At the end of those three weeks, I had exactly one ten-second clip of a hand drawing a line on paper, and a growing sense of failure.

The submission deadline was approaching. I felt the familiar tightening of anxiety. I had a script I believed in and no way to realize it. In a moment of exhausted desperation, I uploaded my script to NotebookLM, a free AI tool I had used once before for summarizing research papers. I didn’t know what I expected. Maybe it would give me feedback. Maybe it would just store the text.

What came back was a podcast. Two AI voices, natural and conversational, discussing my script. They explained the symbolism of the maps. They connected the scenes to adult learning theory more clearly and concisely than I had been able to in my own head. They pointed out implications I hadn’t fully articulated. One voice said to the other:

“You know, his inability to make the film, that’s not a failure. That’s the third pedagogical incident. The system gave him a printed map called ‘make a short film,’ and when that didn’t work, he had to draw his own.”

I listened to it twice, sitting perfectly still.

That moment crystallized something profound. The film I had wanted to make would have been a product, a finished, polished artifact to submit for evaluation. The podcast that emerged by accident was a process, messy, unexpected, and genuinely reflective. And the process, I realized with sudden clarity, was the learning. The obstacle had not been a detour from the learning. The obstacle was the learning, if I chose to see it that way.

I submitted the podcast along with a short reflection. I don’t know what grade I’ll receive. But I know this: that assignment taught me more about learning, creativity, and resilience than any perfectly executed project ever could.

A map in progress, never finished, always ours.

Pedagogy vs. Anthrogogy: A Clearer Picture

By the midpoint of the semester, I began to see that pedagogy and anthrogogy are not merely different teaching methods. They represent fundamentally different understandings of what it means to know something and what it means to be a learner.

Aspect Pedagogy Anthrogogy
Learner role Receiver of knowledge Co-creator of meaning
Starting point Empty slate to be filled Rich with experience, assumptions, and blind spots
Goal Reproduction of correct answers Reflection and transformation of the learner
Teacher role Authority who knows and transmits Facilitator who asks and listens
Success metric Exam scores, correct recall Changed thinking patterns, observable shifts in behavior
Key question "Did you get it right?" "What did you notice? What shifted for you?"
Relationship to error Mistake is failure to be avoided Error is material for deeper understanding
Direction of knowledge One-way: teacher to student Multi-directional: experience, reflection, dialogue

This is not to say that pedagogy has no place. Children need structure, guidance, and foundational knowledge that they cannot discover on their own. But for adults who already carry a lifetime of experiences, a purely pedagogical approach becomes a cage. It treats rich, complex human beings as if they were blank pages, and in doing so, it silences the very resources that could make learning transformative.

A Final Note: A Living, Breathing Framework

Anthrogogy is not just an academic concept I studied for a semester and will now file away. It has become a living, breathing framework that I carry into my daily life. I notice it in small moments and large ones.

When I react automatically to a criticism, defensiveness rising before I’ve even processed the words, I now pause and ask: What am I protecting? What would a more reflective response look like? The pause itself is a victory. Before this course, I didn’t know the pause was possible.

When I feel stuck, on a research problem, in a relationship, in a decision, I no longer assume the obstacle is a sign that I’m on the wrong path. I ask: What is this obstacle teaching me? Is it redirecting me toward something I couldn’t see before? That reframing has saved me from despair more than once.

When I choose what to do next with my education, my career, my time, I now ask myself: Is this my map, or someone else’s? Am I following a route drawn by family expectations, societal pressure, or institutional convenience? Or am I drawing, however tentatively, my own? That question is uncomfortable. It doesn’t always have a clear answer. But asking it is itself an act of anthrogogical living.

Prof. Shree Deepa once said in class, during a discussion about self-talk and inner voices, something that has stayed with me like a koan:

“The most important voice is the one inside your head. Learn to speak to it kindly. Everything else follows from that.”

I didn’t fully understand it then. I understand it more now. And I suspect I will spend the rest of my life deepening that understanding.

The road ahead, ready for my own lines.

Closing Words

This course did not give me a new map. It gave me something far more valuable. It gave me the permission I never knew I needed: the permission to draw my own.

For twenty-three years, I had been following maps drawn by others. Syllabi, curricula, grading schemes, career paths, societal scripts about success. I was good at following them. But I had never stopped to ask whether the destination printed on those maps was a place I actually wanted to go.

Anthrogogy handed me a blank page and a pencil and said, gently but firmly:

You have enough experience now. You know enough. Start drawing. It will be imperfect. You will erase and redraw. The map will never be finished. But it will be yours.

I am still learning to draw. My lines are shaky. I sometimes revert to looking at others’ maps for reassurance. But for the first time, I feel like I am holding the pencil. And that, I have come to believe, is the beginning of all real education.

For that, I will always be grateful to Prof. Shree Deepa and to the University of Hyderabad for making space, in an increasingly rigid academic world, for a course that asks students not just to learn, but to think, and not just to think, but to become.
